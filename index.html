Abstract

Abstract

High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) and 2.5D or stacked-memory organizations are usually motivated by high peak bandwidth, but different workloads convert that peak capability into achieved performance in different ways. This project follows the assigned topic by using DRAMsim3 to simulate DDR4, HBM, and an HMC-based proxy for a 2.5D or stacked-memory design, then comparing their behavior under DRAMsim3's built-in stream-add and random-access workload generators.

The baseline results show that high-bandwidth organizations clearly outperform DDR4 in the evaluated runs: HBM reaches 176.37 GB/s on stream compared with 16.90 GB/s for DDR4, a 10.43× ratio, while random access gives a smaller 3.27× ratio. We then analyze why the advantage changes by sweeping HBM channel count, memory frequency, and transaction queue size. The results show that channel count gives the most consistent bandwidth and latency improvement, frequency scaling saturates under random access, and larger queues mainly help stream throughput while increasing latency.

Finally, an HBM-aware Dynamic Voltage Frequency Scaling (DVFS) extension tests whether unused bandwidth headroom can become energy savings: a queueing-pressure policy saves 21.1% energy with a 20.0% bandwidth change under light load, and saves 2.6% energy with only a 0.6% bandwidth change under mixed load. A supplementary guardband sweep then shows that ε = 0.08 is dominated by ε = 0.02 on the mixed trace, which lifts mixed-load energy savings to 8.6% while keeping P99 read latency unchanged; we therefore recommend ε = 0.02 as the default once a small tail-latency monitor is in place.

Introduction

The Memory Wall

Processors stall not because arithmetic is slow, but because data movement cannot keep up:

\[ T_{\text{total}} = T_{\text{compute}} + T_{\text{memory}} \]

Stream workloads supply locality; random workloads expose scheduling and queueing limits. A workload benefits only from bandwidth that becomes completed service.

Research Question

Which workload properties and architectural variables explain the gap between peak and achieved bandwidth?

\[ U_{\text{bw}} = \frac{B_{\text{achieved}}}{B_{\text{peak}}} \]

This separates three effects: demand parallelism, architectural service parallelism, and queueing pressure — and whether unused headroom can become energy savings.

Approach

Baseline: DDR4 vs HBM vs HMC/2.5D proxy

Sensitivity: channel count, frequency, queue size

Extension: DVFS energy savings from bandwidth slack

Background

DDR4

The conventional memory baseline. Single channel, 64-bit bus, \(t_{CK} = 0.63\) ns. Well-understood timing and mature ecosystem, but limited bandwidth for memory-intensive workloads.

HBM

HBM uses stacked DRAM and a wide interface to provide high aggregate bandwidth. Its key advantage is not only higher frequency, but also greater channel-level parallelism — more channels allow more memory operations to proceed independently, reducing queueing pressure.

2.5D / Stacked Memory

2.5D integration places memory and logic on an interposer, enabling dense connections and high bandwidth between components. We use an HMC configuration as a limited proxy for comparison.

Workloads and Metrics

Stream vs Random

Stream-add (\(C = A + B\)): sequential, bandwidth-intensive accesses with strong row-buffer locality.

Random-access: independent requests with no locality, exposing queueing and scheduling limits.

Peak bandwidth

\[ B_{\text{peak}} = c \cdot \frac{w \cdot \text{BL}}{8 \cdot t_{CK}} \]

\(c\) = channels, \(w\) = bus width (bits), BL = burst length, \(t_{CK}\) = clock period (ns). HBM: 8 × 128 × 4 / (8 × 1) = 512 GB/s.

Access-time model

\[ T_{\text{access}} = T_{\text{queue}} + T_{\text{service}} \]

More channels reduce queueing pressure. Larger queues expose parallelism but can also raise wait time.

Section 5.1

Queueing Theory

M/M/1 — the intuition behind the DVFS policy

In an M/M/1 queue, arrival rate λ and service rate μ determine system utilization ρ:

\[ \rho = \frac{\lambda}{\mu} \]

The expected time in the system is:

\[ W = \frac{1}{\mu - \lambda} = \frac{1}{\mu(1 - \rho)} \]

As ρ → 1, the denominator shrinks and W grows without bound — the system is stable but increasingly slow. This is why the DVFS policy estimates ρ from three orthogonal signals (request throughput, bank idle ratio, average read latency) and lowers frequency when ρ stays low, harvesting unused headroom as energy savings.

The pressure score P is a piecewise function (min() clamps each term to [0, 1]):

\[ P = 0.50 \cdot \min\!\left(\frac{r}{r_{\text{sat}}},\,1\right) + 0.35 \cdot (1 - I) + 0.15 \cdot \min\!\left(\frac{L}{L_{\text{budget}}},\,1\right) \]
What is a Bank? A bank is an independent sub-array inside a DRAM chip. Each bank can be in a different stage of access (row activation, read/write, precharge) or refresh at the same time. This is the key to DRAM parallelism: while one bank is busy, others can serve requests — like a pipeline. HBM has 16 banks (4 ranks × 4 banks/rank), DDR4 typically 16–32. More banks = more parallelism = higher throughput.

Section 5.1

DVFS Design

Setup

Fixed baseline: HBM at 1250 MHz (tCK = 0.80 ns). The dynamic policy picks one of:

  • low: 800 MHz · tCK = 1.25 ns
  • mid: 1000 MHz · tCK = 1.00 ns
  • high: 1250 MHz · tCK = 0.80 ns

The trace is divided into 100,000-cycle windows: each window is simulated twice — once at fixed-high as a pressure probe, then at the DVFS-selected tier.

Target clock and guardband

\[ f_{\text{target}} = \bigl[\,f_{\min} + (f_{\max} - f_{\min}) \cdot P\,\bigr] \cdot (1 + \varepsilon) \]

Then map to the nearest tier (piecewise):

\[ f_{\text{selected}} = \begin{cases} 800 \text{ MHz (low)} & \text{if } f_{\text{target}} \le 800 \\ 1000 \text{ MHz (mid)} & \text{if } 800 < f_{\text{target}} \le 1000 \\ 1250 \text{ MHz (high)} & \text{if } f_{\text{target}} > 1000 \end{cases} \]

Priority traffic raises P to a floor of 0.85, forcing the high tier.

Section 5.1

DVFS Design — Analysis

Why low tier is unreachable

Even at ε = 0 and P = 0, target = 800 MHz → low. But P never reaches 0. The bank-busy term (1−I) has a structural floor: idle ratio I ≈ 0.7 (never 1.0, due to refresh + background activity), so 0.35 × 0.3 ≈ 0.10 is always present. Adding request-rate and latency terms, P ≈ 0.14 → target ≈ 863 MHz → still rounds to mid.

Unlocking low requires changing the pressure score's functional form, not just tuning ε.

BW drop is an artifact, not a real loss

The 20% reported BW drop at mid is explained by the clock ratio: tCK ratio = 1.0/0.8 = 1.25×. The same trace takes 25% more wall-clock time at mid, so reported GB/s falls — but service capacity is not lost.

HBM timings (CL/tRCD/tRP) are nearly identical in nanoseconds across tiers:

\[ 15.0 \;/\; 14.0 \;/\; 14.4 \text{ ns at } 800/1000/1250 \text{ MHz} \]
M/M/1 intuition: Average queue wait time is \[ W_q = \frac{\rho}{\mu(1-\rho)} \]. When ρ → 1, wait → ∞. DVFS must keep μ strictly above λ with enough margin — but μ too large wastes energy.
From our data: HBM baseline peak = 512 GB/s. Stream achieves 176.4 GB/s (ρ ≈ 0.34), random achieves 60.6 GB/s (ρ ≈ 0.12). Light load gives P low → mid tier saves 21.1% energy; heavy load pushes P high → must hold 1250 MHz.

Section 7

Discussion

Three bottleneck categories explain the gap: (1) demand-side parallelism — the workload must provide enough independent requests; (2) service parallelism — channels and banks must be numerous enough; (3) queueing pressure — enough outstanding work, but not so much that latency explodes.

When HBM is available

Prioritize regular, high-throughput tasks on HBM — they can use channel parallelism and wide-interface bandwidth most directly. Irregular random-access tasks still benefit, but should not be expected to scale with peak BW.

Section 8

Conclusion

Baseline

HBM strongly outperforms DDR4: 10.43× stream, 3.27× random. The HMC/2.5D proxy is lower on stream but higher on random.

Sensitivity

Channel count is the strongest lever (6.66× stream, 7.73× random). Frequency raises peak faster than achieved work. Queue size trades stream throughput for latency.

DVFS

Queueing-pressure DVFS harvests slack: 21.1% energy at light load. ε = 0.02 Pareto-dominates ε = 0.08 on mixed: 8.6% energy, P99 unchanged.

Limitations

  • Memory-system results inside DRAMsim3, not full-application speedups
  • HMC config is a proxy, not a physical interposer model
  • Tail-latency result is empirical evidence, not a formal SLA proof
